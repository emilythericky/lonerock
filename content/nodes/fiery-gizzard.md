## Depth

**White-nose syndrome**
Caused by *Pseudogymnoascus destructans*, a cold-tolerant fungus native to Europe. North American bat populations lack evolutionary exposure and therefore immunity. First detected in Schoharie County, New York, 2006. As of 2024, confirmed in 40 US states, 8 Canadian provinces. Species most affected include little brown bat (*Myotis lucifugus*), tricolored bat (*Perimyotis subflavus*), northern long-eared bat (*Myotis septentrionalis*). The northern long-eared bat was listed as endangered in 2023. Tricolored bat listing is pending.

**Cave closure protocols**
The US Fish and Wildlife Service issued decontamination protocols in 2012. Many caves on public land in the Cumberland Plateau are now closed to recreational caving or require decontamination before entry. Caves with significant bat populations may be closed year-round during hibernation season.

**Little Fiery Gizzard Creek**
Flows through the South Cumberland State Park, joining the Collins River. The name: no one is certain of the origin. The trail through Fiery Gizzard Gorge is considered one of the most scenic and difficult trails in Tennessee. It runs through hemlock forest, past waterfalls, through sandstone bluffs. The hemlock woolly adelgid (*Adelges tsugae*) is killing the hemlocks. Another introduced invasive. Another wave of white-nose syndrome scale loss.
