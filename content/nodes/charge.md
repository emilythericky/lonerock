## Surface

**Charge**

When Matteo and I got into that house in the Walking District on Belmont Ave. right near Vanderbilt University Kwik Mike gave us the tip on it said a nurse from Vandy had gone to Sierra Leone and hadn't come back for over seven years but only white or fair or light could walk there let alone git it git git it git we stepped on a lion skin rug so dusty and it peeled apart when we picked it up disintegrated was heavy then was not a pelt blown wide open we go wide open found a bunch of carved mahogany or whatever masks and three big bottles of powdered morphine synthesized in Alabama pretty old but good and a leather bull whip with a wooden handle broke the whip first night a rainy night whipping the stop sign on Belmont gave JayVe the masks stuck it under my tongue and up my nose and in Brigitte's arm up my ass once the morphine for one summer in that brick shotgun right beside Kaycee Homes how Brigitte finally got intubated it was a fungus the powder left on her heart so gummed up the powder we called it Bill's ashes

## Depth

**Kaycee Homes**
A Nashville public housing development, now partially demolished and redeveloped under HOPE VI. Located on the south side of Nashville near Belmont University. As of the poem's timeframe, it would have been a predominantly Black community adjacent to predominantly white university neighborhoods — the geography of Nashville's race and class segregation visible in the adjacency.

**Endocarditis from IV drug use**
Infective endocarditis — infection of the heart's inner lining or valves — is a serious complication of intravenous drug use. *Staphylococcus aureus* is the most common pathogen. Treatment requires weeks of IV antibiotics; severe cases require valve surgery. Mortality rate without treatment: near 100%. With treatment: 25-40% in drug-use-associated cases. Grundy County and surrounding rural Tennessee counties have seen significant increases in endocarditis hospitalizations since 2010.

**Morphine and the Alabama pharmaceutical industry**
Pharmaceutical manufacturing in Alabama, including opioid production, is substantial. The connection between legal pharmaceutical production and the illegal opioid market is not metaphorical — diversion of legally produced opioids into illegal supply chains was extensively documented by DEA investigations in the 2000s-2010s.
